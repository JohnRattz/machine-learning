Implementation: Outlier Detection¶

Detecting outliers in the data is extremely important in the data preprocessing step of any analysis. The presence of outliers can often skew results which take into consideration these data points. There are many "rules of thumb" for what constitutes an outlier in a dataset. Here, we will use Tukey's Method for identfying outliers: An outlier step is calculated as 1.5 times the interquartile range (IQR). A data point with a feature that is beyond an outlier step outside of the IQR for that feature is considered abnormal.

In the code block below, you will need to implement the following:

  • Assign the value of the 25th percentile for the given feature to Q1. Use np.percentile for this.
  • Assign the value of the 75th percentile for the given feature to Q3. Again, use np.percentile.
  • Assign the calculation of an outlier step for the given feature to step.
  • Optionally remove data points from the dataset by adding indices to the outliers list.

NOTE: If you choose to remove any outliers, ensure that the sample data does not contain any of these points!
Once you have performed this implementation, the dataset will be stored in the variable good_data.

outliers = set() # This is a set of indices corresponding to outliers to remove.
outliers_more_than_one_feature = set() # The number of outliers that are outliers in more than one feature.

# For each feature find the data points with extreme high or low values
for feature in log_data.keys():
    
    # Calculate Q1 (25th percentile of the data) for the given feature
    Q1 = np.percentile(log_data[feature], 25)
    
    # Calculate Q3 (75th percentile of the data) for the given feature
    Q3 = np.percentile(log_data[feature], 75)
    
    # Use the interquartile range to calculate an outlier step (1.5 times the interquartile range)
    step = 1.5 * (Q3 - Q1)
    
    # Display the outliers
    print "Data points considered outliers for the feature '{}':".format(feature)
    outliers_df = log_data[~((log_data[feature] >= Q1 - step) & (log_data[feature] <= Q3 + step))]
    new_outliers = set(outliers_df.index) # The outliers for this feature.

Question 6¶

What are the advantages to using a K-Means clustering algorithm? What are the advantages to using a Gaussian Mixture Model clustering algorithm? Given your observations about the wholesale customer data so far, which of the two algorithms will you use and why?

Answer:

The K-Means clustering algorithm scales well with the number of samples (http://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/clustering.html), but it considers points to each be definitely mapped to one label.

The Gaussian Mixture Model clustering algorithm assigns probabilities of having labels to data points based on their distance from those labels' associated means in the feature space - assuming a Gaussian distribution of points for all labels. The main advantage of the Gaussian Mixture Model is that it more accurately preserves the magnitudes of association between the data points and the labels by recording the probabilities of the data points coming from Gaussian distributions associated with those labels.

The Gaussian Mixture Model is likely a better choice for this problem, since we may more easily discover significant clusters than we originally start with compared to K-Means clustering. Specifically, after clustering, we may observe data points with significant probabilities of coming from more than one Gaussian. If there are a significant number of data points that are not strongly associated with one label (i.e. one Gaussian distribution) and if they are associated with some of the same labels, then they may be members of another significant cluster, which indicates that we may benefit from running the algorithm with at least one more cluster to discover more trends in the data.

Imagine that this data was for a grocery store rather than a wholesaler. I propose that it does not seem reasonable to assume that all families purchase the same types of goods in comparable amounts and ratios. For example, not all families buy as much fresh food, milk, detergent, and paper as most other families. To account for the small but non-zero probability that a data point that does not correspond to a high amount of spending on these products is still a family, the Gaussian Mixture Model clustering algorithm retains that probability - whereas the K-Means clustering algorithm does not.

Depending on the problem, the number of clusters that you expect to be in the data may already be known. The silhouette coefficient for a data point measures how similar it is to its assigned cluster from -1 (dissimilar) to 1 (similar). Question 10¶

Companies will often run A/B tests when making small changes to their products or services to determine whether making that change will affect its customers positively or negatively. The wholesale distributor is considering changing its delivery service from currently 5 days a week to 3 days a week. However, the distributor will only make this change in delivery service for customers that react positively. How can the wholesale distributor use the customer segments to determine which customers, if any, would react positively to the change in delivery service?
Hint: Can we assume the change affects all customers equally? How can we determine which group of customers it affects the most?

Answer:

If Segment 0 represents a cafe or restaurant, then it may be worse for Segment 0 to receive fewer, larger deliveries, since they may want to keep their supply of produce fresh, but this may depend on the individual entities since their rules for acceptable freshness of ingredients may vary. They also may not have sufficient space to store enough goods to be able to wait for the deliveries if they only happen 3 times per week.

If Segment 1 represents grocery stores, then it should almost certainly be better for Segment 1 to receive fewer, larger deliveries, since every delivery may occupy a significant amount of manpower of the grocery store and require some tools to be moved from and to their storage locations at the grocery stores (e.g. bringing out dolly carts to move boxes and then returning them). Large grocery stores can afford to have only 3 deliveries per week, since their stocks should be sufficient to easily handle customer demands in the interim.

To test these hypotheses statistically, an A/B test could be run as follows:

A representational sample of customers in both Segment 0 and Segment 1 should be obtained - where "representational" means that the subsets should have similiar properties to the best-fitting Gaussian distributions associated with their segments. The two subsets can then by combined into one subset, since we are primarily interested in the impact on our profits from a change in the delivery service schedule - not on the fractions of the segments that react positively or negatively. These subsets are then split into one control group and one test group. All customers in the control group are kept on the same delivery schedule (5 deliveries per week), but all customers in the test group are supplied using the new delivery schedule (3 deliveries per week).

The magnitude of negative and positive results for the test group would be the net change in the profits derived from the sales made to those customers (if a customer dislikes the change, the net profits for that customer will likely decrease, and vice versa) plus the savings in shipping costs for those customers given the changes in the volumes of goods shipped to them due to the change in the delivery schedule. If the net increase in profits (i.e. the sum of the previously mentioned values for the customers) is significantly higher than the cost of maintining the new delivery schedule along with the old delivery schedule, then the new delivery schedule should be implemented. Note that this A/B test formulation is valid when determining shipping schedules to customer segments rather than to individual customers. If the shipping schedules for individual customers are determined independently of their associated segments, then only those customers that react positively would have their schedules changed - so those customers that react negatively would not be included in the sum associated with determining the net increase in profits.
